\begin{abstract}
Evaluating text-to-speech (TTS) for Nepali requires probing phonological contrasts that English-trained quality metrics cannot detect: a four-way laryngeal distinction (voiceless/voiced $\times$ unaspirated/aspirated) at five places of articulation, the retroflex--dental opposition, oral--nasal vowel contrasts, gemination, and schwa deletion. We present NepTTS-Bench, the first publicly available comprehensive benchmark for Nepali TTS. The benchmark is built around a phonologically-informed stimulus set of 77 minimal pairs targeting these contrasts within 205 sentences total. We evaluate 12 systems spanning commercial cloud services, Nepali-specific providers, open-source models, and human speech baselines. We collect 7,003 human MOS ratings from 207 native Nepali speakers and compare against 6 automated metrics, including SCOREQ auto-MOS and ASR round-trip CER across 5 ASR systems. Our key findings are: (1) the English-trained automated MOS predictor we test (SCOREQ) is unreliable for Nepali, ranking ElevenLabs v3 first ($4.45$) while human raters rank it sixth ($3.47$); (2) Nepali-specific TTS systems are competitive with global cloud services; (3) ASR model selection critically affects evaluation, with Whisper giving unreliable rankings ($\rho = 0.38$) while Chirp2 agrees strongly with human MOS ($\rho = 0.88$); and (4) our fine-tuned Nepali MOS predictor based on IndicWav2Vec achieves Spearman $\rho = 0.66$ with human ratings. We release the complete benchmark as open-source resources, including the sentence sets, evaluation toolkit (\texttt{neptts-eval}), rating application, model checkpoint, and all data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Text-to-speech (TTS) technology has advanced dramatically, with neural models achieving near-human naturalness for high-resource languages. However, for low-resource languages such as Nepali, which is spoken by approximately 32 million people, systematic evaluation remains absent. No standardized benchmark exists for comparing Nepali TTS systems, making it impossible for researchers and practitioners to assess progress or select appropriate systems.

Several systems now claim Nepali support: Microsoft Edge TTS, Google Gemini Flash, ElevenLabs v3, the Nepali-specific TingTing.io, and open-source Piper TTS. However, their relative quality has not been systematically compared by native speakers.

We present NepTTS-Bench with the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The first Nepali MOS predictor, achieving Spearman $\rho = 0.66$ with human ratings by fine-tuning IndicWav2Vec on our collected data.
\item A curated set of 205 sentences with 77 minimal pairs probing the major Nepali phonological contrasts (aspiration, retroflexion, nasalization, gemination, schwa deletion), supplemented with prosodic, lexical, and robustness probes (see Table~\ref{tab:categories}).
\item Evaluation of 12 systems (10 TTS + 2 human baselines) using 6 automated metrics and human MOS ratings from 207 native speakers.
\item Evidence that two widely-used English-trained speech evaluation models fail on Nepali in distinct ways: SCOREQ for MOS prediction misranks the top systems relative to human raters, and Whisper large-v3-turbo for ASR hallucinates severely (median CER above $1.0$ on four of nine TTS systems). This motivates language-specific evaluation.
\item A complete open-source toolkit: benchmark sentences, evaluation CLI (\texttt{neptts-eval}), web-based rating application, and all evaluation data.\footnote{GitHub: \url{https://github.com/Ampixa/neptts-bench}, HuggingFace: \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/ampixa/neptts-bench}}
\end{enumerate}


\section{Related Work}

\subsection{TTS Evaluation}
Mean Opinion Score (MOS) remains the gold standard for TTS evaluation \citep{streijl2016mos}. Automated MOS predictors include UTMOS \citep{saeki2022utmos}, which won the VoiceMOS Challenge 2022 \citep{huang2022voicemos}, SCOREQ \citep{ragano2024scoreq} from NeurIPS 2024, and DNSMOS \citep{reddy2022dnsmos}. However, these models are trained on English data and their cross-lingual reliability is not established for low-resource Indic languages.


\subsection{ASR Round-Trip Evaluation}
ASR round-trip evaluation measures intelligibility by transcribing synthesized speech and computing error rates against the original text. The TTSDS2 benchmark \citep{minixhofer2025ttsds} demonstrated that metric selection significantly affects system rankings. Our work extends this finding by showing that for Nepali, the choice of ASR system is even more critical than for English.

\subsection{Low-Resource Language TTS}
Meta's MMS-TTS \citep{pratap2023mms} covers 1,100+ languages including Nepali. AI4Bharat's Indic Parler-TTS supports Nepali with emotion-specific prompts. The FLEURS multilingual speech benchmark \citep{conneau2022fleurs} provides parallel utterances across 102 languages including Nepali but is oriented toward ASR and speech translation rather than TTS evaluation. No systematic TTS benchmark exists for evaluating Nepali TTS systems specifically.

\subsection{Nepali Speech Resources}
Nepali speech resources include the OpenSLR-54 corpus and fine-tuned Wav2Vec2/XLS-R ASR models \citep{gagan2023xlsr}. The Chirp2 STT model from Google provides strong Nepali transcription. For TTS, no prior work has systematically evaluated available systems. To our knowledge, no publicly released benchmark combines (i) phonologically-informed Nepali stimuli, (ii) native-speaker MOS ratings at scale, (iii) multi-ASR round-trip evaluation, and (iv) an open evaluation toolkit. NepTTS-Bench is the first to do so.

\paragraph{Minimal Pairs.} Nepali has a four-way laryngeal contrast (voiceless unaspirated, voiceless aspirated, voiced unaspirated, voiced aspirated) across five stop and affricate series at the bilabial, dental, retroflex, palatal, and velar places of articulation, a system absent in English \citep{khatiwada2009, pokharel1989}. For example, \textit{k\=am} (work) vs.\ \textit{kh\=am} (envelope) tests the velar aspiration contrast. Several caveats apply. The voiced aspirated (breathy) series is partially merged in casual speech, the retroflex--dental opposition tends to neutralize in coda position, and some contrasts (oral vs.\ nasal vowels, tatsama-driven gemination) carry lower functional load in Standard Nepali than they do in Hindi. Our stimulus set therefore probes the full phonological grid as it is written, and native raters may legitimately treat near-merged contrasts more leniently. We did not include breathy voice on sonorants (aspirated /m/, /n/, /l/), which a more comprehensive phonological audit would add. We designed 77 minimal pairs in which the two sentences differ by exactly one phonological feature. Each pair was audited for grammatical correctness and minimal pair validity. 12 pairs were replaced and 6 corrected during the audit process.

NepTTS-Bench is best understood as an engineering benchmark with phonologically-motivated stimuli rather than as a phonological study of Nepali in its own right. The minimal-pair design lets a TTS evaluator probe specific contrasts that English-trained quality predictors are unlikely to model, but a fully linguistic validation would require a trained phonetician's review of every pair and IPA-level transcription of all stimuli, which we leave to future work.

\paragraph{Additional Categories.} We include sentences testing question intonation (rising pitch patterns), emotional expression, contrastive stress, homograph disambiguation, robustness (tongue twisters, code-mixed text), and common phrases.

Additionally, 160 natural speech utterances from a Chirp2-transcribed Nepali YouTube corpus serve as human ceiling references.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

\paragraph{Human MOS.} Native Nepali speakers rate blinded audio samples on a 1--5 scale via a web application. Raters choose their available time (5--20 minutes) and receive a balanced, randomized set of stimuli across all systems. Same-sentence stimuli are spaced at least 3 positions apart to reduce fatigue. The system identity is hidden.

\paragraph{ASR Round-Trip.} We transcribe all TTS outputs using 5 ASR systems and compute Character Error Rate (CER): Google Chirp2, Meta MMS-1b-all, Wav2Vec2-XLS-R fine-tuned on Nepali, Whisper small, and Whisper large-v3-turbo (via MLX).

\paragraph{Automated MOS.} We use SCOREQ \citep{ragano2024scoreq}, a NeurIPS 2024 model for no-reference speech quality assessment in the synthetic speech domain, using ONNX inference on CPU.

\paragraph{Pair Discrimination.} For minimal pairs, raters hear both sentences from a pair for a given TTS system and judge whether the contrast words sound different (Yes/No).

\paragraph{Spearman Rank Correlation.} We report Spearman's rank correlation coefficient ($\rho$) throughout to quantify agreement between rankings: $\rho = +1$ indicates perfect monotonic agreement, $0$ no relationship, and $-1$ perfect disagreement. Unlike Pearson correlation, $\rho$ operates on ranks rather than raw values. This makes it invariant to the absolute scale of the underlying scores, which is appropriate when comparing metrics with different ranges (e.g., MOS in $[1,5]$ vs.\ CER as a percentage). We follow the convention that $|\rho| < 0.3$ is weak, $0.3$--$0.6$ moderate, $0.6$--$0.8$ strong, and $>0.8$ very strong.

\paragraph{Statistical Inference.} Confidence intervals reported in Table~\ref{tab:human_mos} are normal-approximation Wald intervals on per-system rating means under the assumption of independent ratings. With approximately 34 ratings per rater on average, this assumption underestimates the true uncertainty; a cluster-robust bootstrap clustered by rater and sentence would yield somewhat wider intervals. The qualitative ranking is robust, but close pairs near the top (TingTing Asmita 3.55, Piper 3.49, TingTing Subina 3.48, ElevenLabs v3 3.47) have overlapping CIs and the ordering between them should not be over-interpreted. We do not report inter-rater reliability (Krippendorff's $\alpha$, ICC) in this version of the paper and flag both items as the most important methodological refinements for future work.

\subsection{Whisper Large-v3-Turbo Hallucinates on Nepali}
\label{sec:whisper-large}

We additionally evaluated Whisper large-v3-turbo (via MLX) as our fifth ASR. Its outputs were unusable: median CER exceeded $1.0$ on four of nine TTS systems, with avg CER $7.92$ on gTTS, $2.29$ on Edge TTS Hemkala, and $0.50$--$0.64$ on systems where smaller ASRs achieve $0.10$--$0.18$. CER values above $1.0$ indicate that the hypothesis is longer than the reference, consistent with severe hallucination rather than transcription. Manual inspection of outputs confirms repeated tokens and inserted phrases unrelated to the input audio.

This is a meaningful negative finding. Whisper large-v3-turbo is widely used as a default ASR in research pipelines, and its failure on Nepali extends beyond the simple unreliability we observe for Whisper-small ($\rho = 0.38$ in Table~\ref{tab:correlations}). For low-resource Nepali TTS evaluation, Whisper large-v3-turbo cannot be used at all in its current form. We exclude it from per-system reporting and recommend that future low-resource speech evaluations explicitly characterize ASR hallucination behavior before relying on round-trip CER.

\subsection{NepaliMOS Predictor}

We train the first Nepali-specific MOS predictor by fine-tuning IndicWav2Vec base \citep{javed2022indicwav2vec} on our human ratings:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Architecture:} IndicWav2Vec base (top 4 transformer layers unfrozen) + 2-layer MLP head (768 $\rightarrow$ 256 $\rightarrow$ 1)
\item \textbf{Training:} 7,003 human ratings aggregated to 1,696 utterance-level mean-MOS targets, MSE loss, Adam with differential learning rates (backbone: $10^{-5}$, head: $10^{-4}$), batch size 2
\item \textbf{Split:} stratified 90/10 utterance-level train/validation split by MOS bucket; the held-out validation set contains roughly 170 utterances and is utterance-disjoint from training
\item \textbf{Result:} Spearman $\rho = 0.66$, Pearson $r = 0.64$ on the validation utterances
\end{itemize}

The reported $\rho$ is therefore an utterance-level correlation on roughly 170 held-out samples, not a system-level correlation (n=10) which would have a far wider confidence interval. The split is utterance-disjoint but not system-disjoint, so the predictor can use system-level acoustic cues that may not transfer to TTS systems unseen during training. A leave-one-system-out cross-validation would be the appropriate generalization test and is left to future work.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{SCOREQ Is Unreliable for Nepali}
SCOREQ, trained on English speech quality data, measures audio fidelity (signal clarity, noise levels) but not language-appropriate pronunciation. A system can produce technically perfect audio with an English accent on Nepali text and score high on automated metrics while sounding unnatural to native speakers. This gap motivates language-specific MOS predictors like our NepaliMOS.

\subsection{Nepali-Specific Systems Are Competitive}
TingTing.io, a Nepal-based TTS provider, achieves the highest human MOS among TTS systems (3.55). This demonstrates that language-specific training can compensate for smaller model scale, and has practical implications for applications targeting Nepali speakers.

\subsection{Open-Source Quality}
Piper TTS (3.49) is fully open-source and runs locally without cloud dependency. Its competitive performance (above ElevenLabs and Edge TTS) makes it viable for applications where privacy, cost, or connectivity constraints preclude cloud services.

\subsection{ASR Model Selection}
Our use of 5 ASR systems reveals that metric reliability varies dramatically. For Nepali TTS evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
\item Google Chirp2 is the most reliable ASR for round-trip evaluation
\item Meta MMS provides a good open-source alternative
\item Whisper small produces unreliable rankings (Spearman $\rho = 0.38$ with human MOS) and should not be used alone for Nepali TTS evaluation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}
Our rater pool of 207 speakers may not fully represent Nepal's linguistic diversity in geography, dialect, or L2 status; we treat ``native Nepali speaker'' as self-declared and do not collect demographic metadata in this version. The benchmark covers Standard Nepali stimuli only. The pair-discrimination subtask has only 54 total ratings (under one rating per minimal pair on average), so we do not draw statistical conclusions from it and retain it in the rating application for ongoing data collection. The NepaliMOS predictor reaches utterance-level $\rho = 0.66$ on a held-out validation split but has not been tested under leave-one-system-out cross-validation, which is the appropriate generalization test. We did not run UTMOS or DNSMOS, so the automated-MOS unreliability finding is established only for SCOREQ in our experiments. Several TTS configurations (Table~\ref{tab:tts_configs}) reflect the specific settings we used; alternative voice IDs, prompts, or generation parameters may yield meaningfully different results for the same provider.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented NepTTS-Bench, the first publicly available comprehensive benchmark for Nepali TTS evaluation, with 205 sentences, 77 minimal pairs, 12 systems evaluated by 207 native speakers (7,003 ratings), 6 automated metrics, and the first Nepali MOS predictor (Spearman $\rho = 0.660$). Our key finding is that SCOREQ, the only English-trained automated MOS predictor we evaluate, is unreliable for Nepali, and that whether this result generalizes to UTMOS, DNSMOS, and other English-trained predictors is an open question for future work. This highlights the critical need for language-specific evaluation in low-resource TTS development.

All resources are publicly available: the benchmark sentence set, evaluation toolkit (\texttt{pip install neptts-eval}), rating application, model checkpoint, and evaluation data.
