\begin{abstract}
We present NepTTS-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for evaluating Nepali text-to-speech (TTS) systems. Our benchmark comprises 205 phonologically-informed sentences covering 77 minimal pairs across 9 contrast categories, and evaluates 12 systems spanning commercial cloud services, Nepali-specific providers, open-source models, and human speech baselines. We collect 7,003 human MOS ratings from 207 native Nepali speakers and compare against 6 automated metrics including SCOREQ auto-MOS and ASR round-trip CER using 5 different ASR systems. Our key findings are: (1) automated MOS predictions trained on English are unreliable for Nepali---SCOREQ ranks ElevenLabs v3 first ($4.45$) while human raters rank it sixth ($3.47$); (2) Nepali-specific TTS systems are competitive with global cloud services; (3) ASR model selection critically affects evaluation---Whisper gives essentially random rankings ($\rho = 0.38$) while Chirp2 is reliable ($\rho = 0.88$); and (4) our fine-tuned Nepali MOS predictor based on IndicWav2Vec achieves Spearman $\rho = 0.66$ with human ratings. We release the complete benchmark---sentence sets, evaluation toolkit (\texttt{neptts-eval}), rating application, model checkpoint, and all data---as open-source resources.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Text-to-speech (TTS) technology has advanced dramatically, with neural models achieving near-human naturalness for high-resource languages. However, for low-resource languages such as Nepali---spoken by approximately 32 million people---systematic evaluation remains absent. No standardized benchmark exists for comparing Nepali TTS systems, making it impossible for researchers and practitioners to assess progress or select appropriate systems.

Several systems now claim Nepali support: Microsoft Edge TTS, Google Gemini Flash, ElevenLabs v3, the Nepali-specific TingTing.io, and open-source Piper TTS. But their relative quality has never been systematically compared by native speakers.

We present NepTTS-Bench with the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item A curated sentence set of 205 sentences covering 77 phonological minimal pairs across 9 contrast categories specific to Nepali phonology (aspiration, retroflexion, nasalization, gemination, schwa deletion).
\item Evaluation of 12 systems (10 TTS + 2 human baselines) using 6 automated metrics and human MOS ratings from 207 native speakers.
\item Evidence that automated MOS predictors trained on English systematically disagree with Nepali native speaker preferences, demonstrating the need for language-specific evaluation.
\item The first Nepali MOS predictor, achieving Spearman $\rho = 0.66$ with human ratings by fine-tuning IndicWav2Vec on our collected data.
\item A complete open-source toolkit: benchmark sentences, evaluation CLI (\texttt{neptts-eval}), web-based rating application, and all evaluation data.\footnote{GitHub: \url{https://github.com/Ampixa/neptts-bench}, HuggingFace: \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/ampixa/neptts-bench}}
\end{enumerate}

This work is motivated by AgentShakti (\url{https://agentshakti.xyz}), our business communication platform that uses Nepali TTS for automated voice calls. Selecting the right TTS system for Nepali requires exactly the kind of native-speaker evaluation that NepTTS-Bench provides.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{TTS Evaluation}
Mean Opinion Score (MOS) remains the gold standard for TTS evaluation \citep{streijl2016mos}. Automated MOS predictors include UTMOS \citep{saeki2022utmos}, which won the VoiceMOS Challenge 2022, SCOREQ \citep{ragano2024scoreq} from NeurIPS 2024, and DNSMOS \citep{reddy2022dnsmos}. However, these models are trained on English data and their cross-lingual reliability is not established. The IndicMOS system \citep{udupa2024indicmos} trained on 7 Indian languages showed that language-specific models substantially outperform English-trained predictors on Indic speech, but Nepali was not among its training languages.

\subsection{ASR Round-Trip Evaluation}
ASR round-trip evaluation measures intelligibility by transcribing synthesized speech and computing error rates against the original text. The TTSDS benchmark \citep{minixhofer2025ttsds} demonstrated that metric selection significantly affects system rankings. Our work extends this finding by showing that for Nepali, the choice of ASR system is even more critical than for English.

\subsection{Low-Resource Language TTS}
Meta's MMS-TTS \citep{pratap2023mms} covers 1,100+ languages including Nepali. AI4Bharat's Indic Parler-TTS supports Nepali with emotion-specific prompts. However, no systematic benchmark exists for evaluating these systems specifically on Nepali.

\subsection{Nepali Speech Resources}
Nepali speech resources include the OpenSLR-54 corpus and fine-tuned Wav2Vec2/XLS-R ASR models \citep{gagan2023xlsr}. The Chirp2 STT model from Google provides strong Nepali transcription. For TTS, no prior work has systematically evaluated available systems.

\section{NepTTS-Bench Design}

\subsection{Sentence Set}

Our benchmark comprises 205 sentences designed to test specific aspects of Nepali TTS quality, informed by Nepali phonology \citep{khatiwada2009, pokharel1989, bandhu1971}:

\paragraph{Minimal Pairs.} Nepali has a four-way laryngeal contrast (voiceless unaspirated, voiceless aspirated, voiced unaspirated, voiced aspirated) at five places of articulation---a distinction absent in English. For example, \textit{k\=am} (work) vs.\ \textit{kh\=am} (envelope) tests the velar aspiration contrast. We designed 77 minimal pairs where the two sentences differ by exactly one phonological feature. Each pair was audited for grammatical correctness and minimal pair validity, with 12 pairs replaced and 6 corrected during the audit process.

\paragraph{Additional Categories.} We include sentences testing question intonation (rising pitch patterns), emotional expression, contrastive stress, homograph disambiguation, robustness (tongue twisters, code-mixed text), and common phrases.

Additionally, 160 natural speech utterances from a Chirp2-transcribed Nepali YouTube corpus serve as human ceiling references.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}

\paragraph{Human MOS.} Native Nepali speakers rate blinded audio samples on a 1--5 scale via a web application. Raters choose their available time (5--20 minutes) and receive a balanced, randomized set of stimuli across all systems. Same-sentence stimuli are spaced at least 3 positions apart to reduce fatigue. The system identity is hidden.

\paragraph{ASR Round-Trip.} We transcribe all TTS outputs using 5 ASR systems and compute Character Error Rate (CER): Google Chirp2, Meta MMS-1b-all, Wav2Vec2-XLS-R fine-tuned on Nepali, Whisper small, and Whisper large-v3-turbo (via MLX).

\paragraph{Automated MOS.} We use SCOREQ \citep{ragano2024scoreq}, a NeurIPS 2024 model for no-reference speech quality assessment in the synthetic speech domain, using ONNX inference on CPU.

\paragraph{Pair Discrimination.} For minimal pairs, raters hear both sentences from a pair for a given TTS system and judge whether the contrast words sound different (Yes/No).

Table~\ref{tab:human_mos} presents the main human evaluation results. Key observations:

\textbf{Human ceiling.} Natural speech scores 3.96, not 5.0, reflecting that even human recordings have quality variation (background noise, recording equipment). Read speech (3.62) scores lower than spontaneous speech (3.96), possibly due to the unnatural cadence of prompted reading.

\textbf{Nepali-specific systems compete.} TingTing Asmita (3.55) is the highest-rated TTS system, outperforming global cloud services including ElevenLabs v3 (3.47) and both Edge TTS voices (3.37--3.38).

\textbf{Open-source is competitive.} Piper (3.49), a fully open-source local model, ranks second among TTS systems---above ElevenLabs and Edge TTS.

\textbf{gTTS is unusable.} Google Translate TTS scores 2.58, far below all other systems, confirming that concatenative TTS is inadequate for Nepali.

\subsection{NepaliMOS Predictor}

We train the first Nepali-specific MOS predictor by fine-tuning IndicWav2Vec base \citep{javed2022indicwav2vec} on our human ratings:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Architecture:} IndicWav2Vec base (top 4 transformer layers unfrozen) + 2-layer MLP head (768 $\rightarrow$ 256 $\rightarrow$ 1)
\item \textbf{Training:} 7,003 human ratings, MSE loss, Adam with differential learning rates (backbone: $10^{-5}$, head: $10^{-4}$), batch size 2
\item \textbf{Result:} Spearman $\rho = 0.66$, Pearson $r = 0.64$
\end{itemize}

Performance scales with data volume:

The log-linear scaling suggests that 10,000+ ratings would achieve $\rho > 0.7$, reaching production quality. The jump from 0.587 to 0.660 with just 1,200 additional ratings confirms that data volume, not architecture, is the primary bottleneck.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Automated MOS Is Unreliable for Nepali}
SCOREQ, trained on English speech quality data, measures audio fidelity (signal clarity, noise levels) but not language-appropriate pronunciation. A system can produce technically perfect audio with an English accent on Nepali text and score high on automated metrics while sounding unnatural to native speakers. This gap motivates language-specific MOS predictors like our NepaliMOS.

\subsection{Nepali-Specific Systems Are Competitive}
TingTing.io, a Nepal-based TTS provider, achieves the highest human MOS among TTS systems (3.55). This demonstrates that language-specific training can compensate for smaller model scale, and has practical implications for applications targeting Nepali speakers.

\subsection{Open-Source Quality}
Piper TTS (3.49) is fully open-source and runs locally without cloud dependency. Its competitive performance (above ElevenLabs and Edge TTS) makes it viable for applications where privacy, cost, or connectivity constraints preclude cloud services.

\subsection{ASR Model Selection}
Our use of 5 ASR systems reveals that metric reliability varies dramatically. For Nepali TTS evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
\item Google Chirp2 is the most reliable ASR for round-trip evaluation
\item Meta MMS provides a good open-source alternative
\item Whisper small produces essentially random rankings and should not be used alone for Nepali TTS evaluation
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}
Our rater pool (207 speakers) may not fully represent Nepal's linguistic diversity. The benchmark covers Standard Nepali but not dialectal variations. The pair discrimination test has limited data (54 ratings). The NepaliMOS predictor, while the first of its kind, has not yet reached production-quality correlation.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented NepTTS-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for Nepali TTS evaluation, with 205 sentences, 77 minimal pairs, 12 systems evaluated by 207 native speakers (7,003 ratings), 6 automated metrics, and the first Nepali MOS predictor (Spearman 0.587). Our key finding---that automated MOS predictors are unreliable for Nepali---highlights the critical need for language-specific evaluation in low-resource TTS development.

All resources are publicly available: the benchmark sentence set, evaluation toolkit (\texttt{pip install neptts-eval}), rating application, model checkpoint, and evaluation data.
